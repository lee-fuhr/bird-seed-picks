Bird seed questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Bird seed choices depend on the species you want to attract, not on price or flashy packaging. Black-oil sunflower seed is the single best all-around pick, while millet, nyjer, and suet serve specific birds. The key is matching seed type to feeder style and keeping it fresh and dry.

What type of bird seed attracts the most birds?

Black-oil sunflower seed is the undisputed champion. It has a thin shell that small birds like chickadees, finches, and nuthatches can crack, and its high oil content provides energy year-round. A feeder filled with straight sunflower seed will draw a wider variety of birds than any mixed blend. If you want to attract specific species, add nyjer (thistle) for goldfinches and siskins, white proso millet for ground-feeding sparrows and doves, and suet (rendered fat) for woodpeckers and insect-eaters. Avoid fillers like red milo, wheat, and oats; most birds ignore them, and they rot in the feeder.

How should I store bird seed to keep it fresh?

Store seed in a cool, dry place inside a metal or heavy-duty plastic container with a tight-sealing lid. A galvanized trash can with a locking handle works well and also keeps out rodents and raccoons. Keep the container out of direct sunlight and away from garage floor moisture. Avoid keeping seed in its original paper or thin plastic bag for more than a week. Heat and humidity trigger mold growth and attract pantry moths. If you see webbing or tiny insects, discard the seed immediately and wash the container with hot, soapy water before refilling. For long-term storage, a few food-grade silica gel packets can help control humidity.

Does expensive bird seed really work better?

Not necessarily. Price often reflects marketing and fancy packaging, not the nutritional value for wild birds. Many premium blends advertise “no filler,” which is good, but you can achieve the same result by buying single-ingredient black-oil sunflower seed in bulk from a farm store or feed co-op at a much lower cost per pound. That said, there is a case for buying fresh seed from a high-volume dealer. A small boutique brand that sells seed that has been stored properly and turned over quickly may offer better freshness, which birds notice. But for most people, a standard sack of sunflower seed from a reputable store is perfectly fine and much more budget-friendly.

What should I look for in a bird seed blend?

Read the ingredient list, not just the front label. A good blend lists sunflower seed (black-oil or striped), white proso millet, and cracked corn as primary ingredients. Millet is fine; it’s a favorite of doves and juncos. Avoid blends that list “milo,” “wheat,” “oats,” or “buckwheat” near the top, these are cheap fillers that most birds will kick out of the feeder. If you’re new to bird feeding, start with a single ingredient like black-oil sunflower or a “no mess” mix (hulled sunflower and shelled peanuts) to avoid waste. Once you know what species visit your yard, you can customize with specialty seeds like nyjer for finches or safflower for cardinals and to discourage squirrels.

Can feeding birds in the summer be harmful?

No, not if you manage it well. Birds need high-energy food during nesting and molting season just as much as in winter. The real risk is mold. Hot, humid weather can turn seed moldy in a few days, so feed only as much as birds will eat in 24 to 48 hours. Use a feeder with drainage holes and a removable tray for easy cleaning. Clean your feeder every two weeks with a mild bleach solution (1 part bleach to 9 parts water), rinse thoroughly, and dry before refilling. Provide a clean water source nearby, like a shallow birdbath changed daily, to help birds stay hydrated during heat waves.

Is it okay to feed birds in the winter?

Absolutely, winter feeding can be critical when natural food sources like insects and berries are scarce. Focus on high-fat, high-calorie options: black-oil sunflower seed, suet cakes, shelled peanuts, and nyjer. These give birds the energy to shiver through cold nights. Make sure feeders stay dry. Snow and rain can clog feeding ports and cause seed to freeze in the tray. Choose feeders with roofs or place them under an overhang. If you live in a deep-snow region, keep a small snow-free patch beneath the feeder by brushing it clear daily, that’s where ground-feeding birds like cardinals and sparrows will pick up spilled seed.

Frequently asked questions

What’s the cheapest bird seed that still works well?

Plain black-oil sunflower seed sold in bulk is usually the most cost-effective option. It attracts the most species and has almost no filler waste. A 50-pound sack from a farm supply store often costs less per pound than a small bag of premium mix from a big-box store.

How often should I clean my bird feeder?

Clean tube feeders and hopper feeders every two weeks, and more often in wet or humid weather. Nyjer feeders and suet cages need monthly cleaning because they can grow mold quickly. Let everything dry completely before refilling to keep seed fresh.

Why are birds kicking seed out of my feeder?

Most likely they are sorting through a cheap mix to find the sunflower or millet and flicking out milo, wheat, or oats they don’t want. Switch to a single-ingredient seed (like black-oil sunflower) or a blend with no fillers, and waste will drop dramatically.

Does bird seed go bad?

Yes. Stale or damp seed can develop mold that produces aflatoxins, which are harmful to birds. Seeds that have been stored for more than a year lose much of their oil content and nutritional value. Buy from a store with high turnover and use it within a few months.

What seed keeps squirrels away?

No seed is squirrel-proof, but safflower seed is a good deterrent because most squirrels dislike its bitter taste. Cardinals, chickadees, and grosbeaks love it. Also try adding cayenne pepper to sunflower seed (birds don’t taste capsaicin, but mammals do) or using a squirrel-proof feeder design.

Can I feed birds bread or table scraps?

It’s not recommended. Bread offers little nutrition and can fill birds up without providing the energy they need. Moldy bread is especially dangerous. Stick to bird seed, suet, or fresh fruit like orange halves or grapes for orioles and tanagers.

In shortChoosing the right bird seed really comes down to knowing your local birds and feeding them what they actually eat. Start with black-oil sunflower, keep your seed dry and fresh, and clean feeders on a regular schedule. That straightforward routine will bring more birds to your yard, and keep them healthier, than any fancy blend or gadget ever could.
